Our History

History of UBC Chem-E-Car

Updated August 2015

The team started out in the 2008/2009 school year due to the AIChE student conference being held at UBC that year. Some faculty members and students decided to create a team to participate in the ongoing competition. That year, the team used two power sources in parallel for the vehicle: a 5-cell hydrogen-air fuel cell and a 5-cell zinc-air battery. This car won the 2nd place in the poster competition for safety considerations and uniqueness of idea, as it was the team’s first time participating in the competition.

During the next school year, the team entered the 8th World Congress of Chemical Engineering, which took place in Montreal. It shifted from using fuel cells to pressure vessels. A second group was added to the team; these two groups were formed to create and develop two different ideas. The better idea would then be chosen and used for the competition. As the team grew, it gained a better reputation and more funding within and outside of UBC.

In 2013, the team entered the AIChE Chem-E-Car competition with its “Dehydrated Water Mobile”. The students used a reaction of sodium borohydride and water to produce hydrogen, which was used to power the vehicle. This car was awarded 2nd place at the Pacific Northwest Regional Conference and placed 6th at the National competition.

In 2014, zinc-air batteries were used as fuel cells to power the vehicle. Also, a sodium thiosulphate clock was used as the stopping mechanism. The 2014 vehicle “Plastic Metals” won the Regional competition. Competing against 35 other teams from USA, Puerto Rico, Qatar, Poland, South Korea and Malaysia, the team placed 8th out of 36 teams in the National Competition. In 2013 and 2014, UBC Chem-E-Car was the only Canadian team to qualify for the AIChE National Chem-E-Car Competition.

The team has been involved in several outreach events to high schools showing our designs. This helps to get the younger students interested in science and technology related fields. The importance of alternative energy is usually discussed. The students are also shown chemical reactions that take place in the car, such as the stopping mechanism.

Since its establishment, the team has helped UBC students develop a multidisciplinary skill set. Students learn different aspects of being in the team from the mechanical aspects of putting together the drive train to the chemical aspects of testing batteries. Other soft skills such as working in a team and fundraising are also developed.

With the world's growing interest in clean energy production and storage, it is indeed an exciting time to be part of UBC Chem-E-Car.
